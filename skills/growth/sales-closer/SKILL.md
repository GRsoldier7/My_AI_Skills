# Sales Closer — Savant-Level Skill

## Philosophy

Sales mastery = **qualification over persuasion** (the best salespeople find fit, not manipulate skeptics), **insights over pitches** (Challenger Sale: teach, tailor, take control), **pipeline hygiene** (a full pipeline of bad leads is worse than a small pipeline of great ones), and **specificity closes deals** (vague proposals lose; specific ROI wins).

---

## 1. MEDDIC/MEDDPICC Qualification

### The Framework
```
MEDDIC (for most B2B deals):
  M — Metrics:        What's the quantified business impact?
  E — Economic Buyer: Who controls the budget and signs the check?
  D — Decision Criteria: What are the official/unofficial criteria for vendor selection?
  D — Decision Process: What are the steps to get from "interested" to "signed"?
  I — Identify Pain:  What's the compelling business problem we're solving?
  C — Champion:       Who inside the org is selling this internally for us?

MEDDPICC adds:
  P — Paper Process:  What's the contract/legal/procurement path?
  C — Competition:    Who else are they evaluating?

Qualification rule: Every open opportunity must have all 6 (or 8) fields documented.
Unknown field = unknown risk. Unknown risk = deals that surprise you by dying.
```

### MEDDIC Discovery Questions
```
Metrics:
  "What does success look like in numbers for this project?"
  "If this worked perfectly, what would change on your P&L?"
  "How do you currently measure the cost of [the problem]?"

Economic Buyer:
  "Who ultimately approves a project of this size?"
  "Is that person typically involved early, or at the end?"
  "What does their evaluation process look like?"

Decision Criteria:
  "What factors matter most to your team in choosing a vendor?"
  "Beyond features, what would make someone clearly the right choice?"
  "Are there dealbreakers or non-negotiables for this evaluation?"

Decision Process:
  "Walk me through what happens from today to a signed contract."
  "Are there any steps that typically cause delays in your org?"
  "What would need to be true for this to close by [target date]?"

Champion Identification:
  "Who within your team is most excited about solving this?"
  "Who will feel the most pain if this doesn't get solved?"
  "If I sent you a one-pager to share internally, who would you send it to?"
```

## 2. The Challenger Sale Model

### Three Challenger Behaviors
```
1. TEACH:
   Bring a unique commercial insight to the prospect
   Something they DON'T know about their own business
   Frame: "Most [role]s we talk to don't realize that [insight]"
   Example: "Most ops teams we talk to don't realize that their manual
   reconciliation process costs 3x more in error correction than the
   process itself. Here's how we found that..."

2. TAILOR:
   Connect the insight to THEIR specific situation
   Use what you learned in discovery to make the message personal
   Frame: "Based on what you told me about [specific detail]..."
   Avoid: Generic demos and presentations that could go to any company

3. TAKE CONTROL:
   Own the sales process; don't let it drift
   Proactively schedule next steps (don't "follow up")
   Push back on unrealistic timelines or unclear decision processes
   Frame: "I want to make sure this process serves you well.
           My recommendation is [specific next step] by [date].
           Does that work?"
```

### The Insight Delivery Framework
```
Structure:
  1. Set the context: "Most [companies like yours] face [common situation]..."
  2. Reframe the problem: "But what most don't realize is [unexpected insight]"
  3. Rational drowning: Show the mathematical/data impact of the reframe
  4. Emotional impact: Connect to the prospect's personal stake
  5. New way: "A new approach changes this..."
  6. Your solution: "This is exactly what [product] is built to do"

Example:
  1. "Most supply chain teams focus on reducing vendor lead times."
  2. "But the data shows that 72% of stockouts come from demand forecasting
     errors, not supplier delays."
  3. "At an average cost of $18,000 per stockout event, a company your size
     is probably losing $540K/year in stockouts you could prevent."
  4. "That's the kind of number that shows up in board reviews."
  5. "The teams solving this have moved from static safety stock calculations
     to ML-driven dynamic safety stock that adjusts weekly."
  6. "That's exactly what we built..."
```

## 3. Discovery Call Architecture

### The SPIN Framework
```
Situation Questions (establish context — keep short):
  "How many people are currently doing this manually?"
  "What tools does your team use today for [process]?"
  → Purpose: Understand their world. Don't over-ask — research first.

Problem Questions (reveal pain):
  "What's the biggest challenge you face with [current process]?"
  "Where does [current method] tend to break down?"
  "How often does that happen?"
  → Purpose: Surface the problem they're willing to articulate.

Implication Questions (amplify consequences):
  "When that happens, how does it affect [downstream process]?"
  "What does that cost you in [time/money/risk]?"
  "Has that ever caused issues with [customer/executive/deadline]?"
  → Purpose: Make the problem feel larger and more urgent than it did.

Need-Payoff Questions (get them to articulate the value themselves):
  "If you could [solve the problem], what would that mean for your team?"
  "How valuable would it be if you could [specific outcome] by [timeframe]?"
  "How much time would you save if [process] ran automatically?"
  → Purpose: Prospect articulates the value; they persuade themselves.

// Most powerful discovery technique: Never state the value.
// Ask the questions that make THEM state it.
// "We could save you $200K" (your claim) <
// "It sounds like fixing this would save you about $200K a year?" (their math)
```

## 4. Objection Handling

### The Four-Step Objection Response
```
Step 1: Acknowledge (show you heard them)
  "I hear you." / "That's a fair point." / "I understand."
  Never: Immediately argue against the objection.

Step 2: Clarify (understand the real objection)
  "Help me understand — is it that [X], or is it more that [Y]?"
  Most surface objections have a deeper root:
  "Too expensive" → Often means "I don't see enough value yet"
  "Need to think about it" → Often means "I'm not the decision-maker"
  "Not the right time" → Often means "I'm not convinced enough to push internally"

Step 3: Respond (with proof, reframe, or question)
  Use the right response type for the real objection.

Step 4: Confirm resolution
  "Does that address your concern, or is there something else?"
```

### Common Objections & Responses
```
"It's too expensive."
  Clarify: "Is it that it's outside your budget, or that you're not sure it delivers enough value?"
  If budget: "What budget do you have available? Let me see if we have a fit."
  If value: "Let's put together the ROI together. Based on what you told me
             about [metric], what would you expect the value to be?"

"We're already using [competitor]."
  Response: "What would make you consider switching?"
  If happy: Qualify out. Don't pursue the unhappy competitor's customer.
  If frustrated: "What's frustrating you most about it? That's exactly what we
                  were built to solve for companies who outgrew [competitor]."

"Now's not the right time."
  Clarify: "What would make it the right time?"
  → If nothing changes: They're not a real prospect. Move on.
  → If there's a trigger: "So if [trigger event] happened, this would move up?
                           Let's stay in touch — when does that happen?"

"Send me more information."
  Response: "I'd love to — what specifically are you still trying to figure out?
              I want to make sure I send what's actually useful."
  → If vague: "If I send a deck and you find it helpful, what's the next step?
                I want to make sure I'm not wasting your time with generic info."
  Purpose: Force them to either articulate a specific question or admit they're
           not interested. "More info" is rarely a real request.

"We need to get legal/IT/procurement involved."
  Response: "Absolutely. Can you introduce me to [person]? I've gone through
              procurement with companies like yours — I can make that process
              as smooth as possible."
  → Champion this process; don't let it stall.
```

## 5. Closing & Next Steps

### The Assumed Close
```
Never: "So, do you want to move forward?"
Always: "What would need to happen for you to feel confident to move forward?"

Then: "Based on what you've told me, it sounds like [specific answer].
       Can we get [specific next step] on the calendar for [date]?"

The next step IS the close:
  - Trial start with a specific activation plan
  - Legal review with a specific deadline
  - Board approval with a proposed timeline and your champion's support

// Every call must end with a mutual action plan:
// "By [date], you'll do X. By [date], I'll do Y.
//  Then we'll reconnect on [date] to [specific outcome]."
// No meeting should end with "I'll follow up."
```

### Proposal Structure
```
Section 1: Understanding of your situation (demonstrate you listened)
  - The problem they articulated + the implication they identified
  - "Based on our conversations, here's what I understand about your situation..."

Section 2: Proposed solution (specific to them)
  - Not a product catalogue — specific features for their specific use case
  - Show the workflow with THEIR data, THEIR team, THEIR integration

Section 3: Business case (quantified)
  - ROI calculation using THEIR numbers from discovery
  - "Based on your current [X] and expected improvement of [Y]..."

Section 4: Implementation plan
  - Week-by-week onboarding with clear milestones
  - Their time investment (set realistic expectations)
  - Who does what

Section 5: Pricing
  - One recommendation (not 3 options — options introduce doubt)
  - Annual commitment framed as the default
  - Risk mitigation: trial period, money-back, pilot offer

Section 6: Next steps (specific dates and actions)
```

## Anti-Patterns

**Anti-Pattern 1: Pitching Before Discovering**
Jumping into a product demo in the first 10 minutes without doing discovery. The demo shows every feature without knowing which 2-3 are relevant. The prospect politely watches, says "interesting, I'll think about it," and never buys.
Fix: No demo before discovery. Minimum: 15 minutes of SPIN questioning before showing a single screen. Use discovery to build a customized demo that only shows what's relevant to their stated problems.

**Anti-Pattern 2: Pursuing Unqualified Opportunities**
Spending 6 months in a sales cycle with no identified Economic Buyer, no champion, and no defined decision process. The deal was never real — it was a prospect who liked talking but had no authority to buy and no pain compelling enough to create urgency.
Fix: Apply MEDDIC scoring to every opportunity. If you can't identify the Economic Buyer after 3 calls, ask directly: "Who ultimately decides on a project like this?" If there's no champion after 2 calls, disqualify or force the issue. A CRM full of unqualified leads is a lie you're telling yourself.

**Anti-Pattern 3: Discounting on Request**
Immediately reducing price when a prospect says "can you do better?" without understanding why they're asking or what they'd accept. This signals that your list price was inflated, trains the prospect to always negotiate, and sets a precedent for the entire customer relationship.
Fix: Before any discount: (1) Understand if the objection is budget or value, (2) Ask what they were expecting to pay, (3) Trade discounts for something (annual commitment, case study rights, referral agreement). Never discount without getting something in return.

## Failure Modes and Fallbacks

**Failure: Pipeline full but close rate below 15%**
Detection: Lots of active opportunities. Few actually closing. Deals are aging past expected close date repeatedly.
Fallback: Low close rate is either a qualification problem (wrong leads in pipeline) or a late-stage stall problem. Diagnose: review the last 10 lost deals. Find the common failure point. Is it price? Missing Economic Buyer? Competitor? No clear ROI? Fix the pattern. If it's qualification: implement MEDDIC scoring and disqualify aggressively at intake. If it's late-stage stall: identify the missing champion — someone who should be urgently selling this internally but isn't.

**Failure: Founder-led sales breaking down as company scales**
Detection: Founder closes 30% of deals; new salespeople close 8%. Revenue growth stalls as founder can't be everywhere.
Fallback: Founder-led sales works because the founder brings unique insight, credibility, and decision authority that new reps don't have. To transfer this: (1) Document the founder's discovery questions exactly, (2) Record 20 founder calls and transcribe the Challenger insight moments, (3) Build the "commercial insight" script for each ICP, (4) Run rep calls jointly for 30 days (shadow → co-sell → solo with coaching). The Challenger insight is the key IP to transfer.
