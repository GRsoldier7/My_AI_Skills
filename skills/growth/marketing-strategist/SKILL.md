# Marketing Strategist — Savant-Level Skill

## Philosophy

Marketing mastery = **positioning before tactics** (the right message to the right person beats any channel), **specificity over reach** (one sentence that makes the right person say "that's exactly my problem" > broad awareness), and **systems over campaigns** (build machines that compound, not events that spike and die).

---

## 1. Positioning (April Dunford Framework)

### Obviously Awesome Positioning Process
```
Step 1: Find your best customers
  - Who loves you? Not just uses you — genuinely loves you.
  - These are the only customers your positioning should serve.
  - "If I could only have customers like these, what would I call them?"

Step 2: Define competitive alternatives
  - NOT who your competitors are — what would customers DO if you didn't exist?
  - "Do nothing" is a competitor. "Spreadsheet" is a competitor. "Hire someone" is a competitor.
  - This determines the true frame of reference (NOT your chosen category).

Step 3: Isolate your unique attributes
  - What can you do that the competitive alternative cannot?
  - Be specific: "10x faster" (weak), "processes 10M rows in <3 seconds vs. Excel's 4-min timeout" (strong)

Step 4: Map attributes to customer value
  - For each unique attribute, ask: "So what? What does that mean for the customer?"
  - Chain: Feature → Capability → Business value → Emotional value

Step 5: Define your best-fit segments
  - Who values those features the most AND has the pain the most?
  - Not "all SMBs" — "Series A SaaS companies with a data team and a CFO who reports to board"

Step 6: Define the market frame of reference
  - What category best activates the right value associations?
  - Category choice changes everything: "project management" vs. "dev workflow automation"

Positioning statement formula:
  For [best-fit segments]
  Who want to [job to be done / desired outcome]
  [Your product] is a [market category]
  That [unique value attributes]
  Unlike [competitive alternatives]
  [Your product] [key differentiator]
```

### Messaging Hierarchy
```
Level 1: Tagline (5-8 words)
  The emotional hook. What you stand for.
  Example: "The fastest way to ship without breaking things."

Level 2: Value prop (1-2 sentences)
  Who it's for + what pain + how you solve it + unique proof.
  Example: "Helix generates personalized health protocols for self-optimizers by
  connecting bloodwork, supplements, and biomarker data. No more guessing —
  just evidence-based protocols built on your data."

Level 3: Elevator pitch (30 seconds)
  Problem → Solution → Proof → Call to action

Level 4: Full story (landing page / pitch)
  Full narrative arc: customer before → the problem → our solution →
  how it works → proof → who it's for → pricing → CTA

// Message testing: Run 5 variants of your tagline/VP in LinkedIn posts.
// Measure: Comments + DMs + "that's so me" reactions. Winner becomes your copy.
```

## 2. The Marketing Funnel

### Full-Funnel Model
```
TOFU (Top of Funnel) — Awareness:
  Goal: Get in front of your ICP
  Channels: SEO, social content, paid awareness, PR, podcast guesting
  Metric: Qualified impressions, email subscribers, community followers

MOFU (Middle of Funnel) — Consideration:
  Goal: Turn aware prospects into engaged evaluators
  Channels: Lead magnets, webinars, case studies, comparison content
  Metric: Email engagement rate, trial signups, content downloads

BOFU (Bottom of Funnel) — Conversion:
  Goal: Convert evaluators to paying customers
  Channels: Free trial, demo, sales call, pricing page, checkout optimization
  Metric: Trial-to-paid conversion rate, sales call close rate, CAC

Retention / Expansion:
  Goal: Keep customers and grow revenue per customer
  Channels: Onboarding emails, success check-ins, feature announcements
  Metric: Net Revenue Retention (NRR), upsell rate, NPS

Referral / Advocacy:
  Goal: Turn customers into your best marketing channel
  Channels: Referral program, case studies, testimonials, community
  Metric: Referral rate, % of new revenue from referrals
```

### Marketing Investment Allocation (Stage-Based)
```
Stage 1 (Pre-PMF):
  TOFU: 10% (minimum — stay discoverable)
  MOFU: 20% (build the email list of the right people)
  BOFU: 70% (convert, learn, iterate)

Stage 2 (Post-PMF, <$1M ARR):
  TOFU: 30%
  MOFU: 30%
  BOFU: 40%

Stage 3 (Scaling, $1M+ ARR):
  TOFU: 40% (compound brand investment)
  MOFU: 30%
  BOFU: 20%
  Referral: 10%

// Rule: Until you have PMF and know your conversion rates, every $1 spent
// on TOFU is a $1 wasted. BOFU first. Always.
```

## 3. Channel Mix & Attribution

### Attribution Models
```
Last-Touch Attribution:
  Gives 100% credit to the last channel before conversion
  Use when: Simple, single-channel funnels
  Problem: Ignores the channels that created awareness

First-Touch Attribution:
  Gives 100% credit to the first channel that touched the customer
  Use when: Long sales cycles; want to credit demand gen
  Problem: Ignores everything that helped close the deal

Linear Attribution:
  Equal credit across every touchpoint
  Use when: Multiple channels all contribute meaningfully
  Better than single-touch but not perfect

// Best practice for early-stage:
// Track first-touch AND last-touch. Compare. Where do they diverge?
// The gap is where your most important mid-funnel channel lives.

Data-Driven Attribution (when you have enough data):
  Use Google Analytics 4's data-driven model
  Requires: 400+ conversions/month to be reliable
```

### Channel ROI Framework
```
For each active channel, track monthly:

Channel    | Spend  | Leads | Conv% | Customers | CAC   | LTV:CAC
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SEO        | $2,000 |  150  |  4%   |     6     | $333  |  3.6x
LinkedIn   | $3,000 |   45  |  9%   |     4     | $750  |  1.6x ❌
Email      | $500   |  200  |  6%   |    12     |  $42  | 28.6x ✅
Cold Email | $1,000 |   60  |  3%   |     2     | $500  |  2.4x ⚠️

// Decision: Double email, evaluate LinkedIn (1.6x is below 3:1 threshold)
// Rule: CAC Payback > 12 months OR LTV:CAC < 3:1 → pause and fix before scaling
```

## 4. B2B vs B2C Marketing Differences

```
B2B Marketing:
  Buying cycle:    Weeks to months
  Decision makers: 3-7 people typically involved
  Content focus:   ROI, case studies, technical credibility
  Channels:        LinkedIn, email, direct outreach, events, partnerships
  Hook:            Professional outcomes and risk reduction
  Proof:           Named customer logos, specific metrics, analyst coverage

B2C Marketing:
  Buying cycle:    Seconds to days
  Decision makers: One person (emotion-driven)
  Content focus:   Desire, identity, FOMO, community belonging
  Channels:        TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Pinterest, paid social
  Hook:            Identity and aspiration ("be the person who...")
  Proof:           Social proof quantity (reviews, UGC, before/afters)

SMB (hybrid):
  Buying cycle:    Days to 2 weeks
  Decision makers: 1-2 (owner/CEO decides fast)
  Content focus:   Time savings + money savings (practical ROI)
  Channels:        LinkedIn, Google Search, email, podcasts
  Hook:            "Do more with fewer people"
  Proof:           Specific time/money saved, peer testimonials
```

## 5. Marketing Systems (Not Campaigns)

### The Perpetual Marketing Machine
```
1. Content Engine (weekly output):
   1 long-form piece (2,000+ word blog or YouTube video)
   → Repurposed into 5 LinkedIn posts
   → Repurposed into 3 Twitter/X threads
   → Repurposed into 1 newsletter
   → Repurposed into 3 short-form clips (TikTok/Reels/Shorts)

2. SEO Compound Engine:
   - Publish 2-4 topical authority articles per month
   - Each article targets 1 high-intent keyword
   - Internal link strategy: Pillars link to clusters; clusters link to product
   - Result: Compounding organic traffic over 6-18 months

3. Email Nurture Machine:
   - Welcome sequence: 5-7 emails over 14 days
   - Weekly newsletter: consistent value delivery
   - Product updates: 2-4x/month
   - Re-engagement: 60-day inactive segment

4. Referral Flywheel:
   - Activate 30 days after signup (not at signup)
   - Incentive: Both sides rewarded (not just referrer)
   - Friction: 1 click to share, not a form
```

## Anti-Patterns

**Anti-Pattern 1: Positioning as Product Feature List**
Writing positioning statements or website copy that lists features ("We have X, Y, Z features") instead of outcomes and differentiation. Feature lists don't differentiate — every competitor claims the same features.
Fix: Use the April Dunford positioning process. Start with your best customers' desired outcomes and your unique ability to deliver them. Features are proof of the promise, not the promise itself.

**Anti-Pattern 2: Marketing Without ICP Precision**
Running ads or content targeting "small business owners" or "people interested in productivity." Generic targeting = generic results. The economics don't work because message-to-market match is poor.
Fix: Get specific to the point it feels too narrow. "Independent financial advisors with $50M-$500M AUM, solo or 2-person practices, using Redtail CRM, in the US" is a real ICP. Build every piece of content for that specific person. When they see it, they'll say "this is exactly for me" — the most powerful marketing sentence possible.

**Anti-Pattern 3: Campaign Mentality Over System Thinking**
Running one-time campaigns (Product Hunt launch, Black Friday discount, PR push) and calling it a marketing strategy. Campaigns spike metrics temporarily but don't compound. Marketing as events creates boom-bust cycles.
Fix: Build systems that produce outputs every week without a major effort investment. SEO compounds over time. A weekly newsletter builds an audience that compounds. A referral program creates passive customer acquisition. The goal is a marketing machine you step away from and return to find more customers.

## Quality Gates

- [ ] Positioning statement written using April Dunford framework (not "we're the best X for Y")
- [ ] Messaging hierarchy complete: tagline, value prop, elevator pitch, full story
- [ ] Funnel stage investment allocation appropriate for current stage (BOFU-heavy pre-PMF)
- [ ] Attribution model defined and tracking implemented (first-touch AND last-touch minimum)
- [ ] Channel ROI table maintained: all active channels scored on CAC and LTV:CAC monthly
- [ ] Marketing machine in place: content engine, email sequence, referral program

## Failure Modes and Fallbacks

**Failure: Marketing generates traffic but no conversions**
Detection: Website traffic is growing. Trial signups or purchases are flat. Bounce rate > 70%.
Fallback: Traffic-without-conversion is a message-to-market mismatch or an offer problem, not a traffic problem. Run 5 user interviews with people who visited and didn't convert. Ask: "What did you come looking for? What did you find instead?" The gap between those answers is your copy problem. Fix the landing page message before buying more traffic.

**Failure: Content strategy producing content no one reads**
Detection: Blog posts indexed, socials posting regularly, but zero engagement. No email growth. No inbound leads.
Fallback: Content failure is almost always an ICP problem, not a quality problem. If the right people aren't engaging, either (a) they're not finding it (distribution failure) or (b) it's not solving their specific problem (relevance failure). Run: Search console review (are the right keywords driving impressions?), community observation (what questions does your ICP actually ask?), and competitor content analysis (what's getting shared in your niche?). Then rebuild the content calendar around demonstrated demand, not assumed interest.
